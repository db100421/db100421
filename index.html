Ingredients

  • 4 rolls of sushi-seaweed (nori)
  • 1 cup uncooked sushi rice
  • 1 cup (8 fl oz) cold water
  • 2 tablespoons sugar
  • 5 tablespoons mirin or rice vinegar
  • Meat and/or vegetable of your choice (e.g., 113g smoked salmon, cucumber, avocado)
  • Soy sauce and pickled ginger (optional)

Making Sushi in 9 Easy Steps

Making sushi is quick and fun! Here's how to do it:

  1. Cook rice in water until it boils. Cover and simmer until grains are soft. Remove from heat and cool.
  2. Chop your fillings (e.g., smoked salmon, cucumber, avocado) into strips or sticks.
  3. Mix sugar and vinegar and combine it with cooled rice for flavor and stickiness.
  4. Place a sheet of nori on your bamboo sushi mat.
  5. Spread a thin layer of the rice mixture over the nori, leaving a 1-inch strip at the top.
  6. Place your filling ingredients in the center horizontally.
  7. Roll the sushi tightly using the bamboo mat (avoid rolling the mat itself).
  8. Wet the top strip of nori and press it to seal the roll.
  9. Cut the roll into 1¼" slices and repeat with the remaining sheets of nori.

Pro Tip:

You will need a special bamboo sushi rolling mat, which you can buy with the nori.

History of Sushi

The origins of sushi trace back to paddy fields where fish was fermented with vinegar, salt, and rice. The rice was initially discarded. The earliest form of sushi, known as "narezushi," was created in Japan during the Yayoi period (early Neolithic–Iron Age). During the Edo period (1603–1867), vinegar replaced fermented rice.

The inventor of modern sushi is believed to be Hanaya Yohei, who introduced "nigiri-zushi" around 1824. Nigiri-zushi consists of seafood placed on hand-pressed vinegared rice and became a popular fast food among the Edo period's chōnin class.

The spread of narezushi began in Southeast Asia, specifically in the Mekong River basin (now Laos, Cambodia, Thailand) and the Irrawaddy River basin (now Myanmar). It eventually became popular in Japan, where rice cultivation and fish fermentation methods evolved.

Important Warning

Rice sometimes contains spores of bacteria that cause food poisoning. These spores can survive cooking, so if rice is left standing at room temperature, harmful bacteria can multiply. Always store cooled rice at less than 4°C and refrigerate sushi if not consumed immediately.

Final Thoughts

Your sushi will be safe to eat on the day you make it. If you're storing it for later, keep it in the refrigerator for freshness!
